# Project Risk and Safety Governance

## Responsible AI Business Architecture

> The project must follow the governance principles it recommends to others.

---

# Purpose

This document defines risk awareness and safety governance principles for the creation, promotion, and evolution of the Responsible AI Business Architecture project itself.

The objective is to ensure that the project remains:

- trustworthy;
- controllable;
- transparent;
- human-accountable;
- resistant to manipulation;
- aligned with its own governance-first principles.

---

# Core Principle

A project about governable autonomy must itself be governed carefully.

---

# Key Risk Awareness

As the project becomes more visible, useful, and connected to AI governance ecosystems, it may encounter risks such as:

- malicious collaboration attempts;
- prompt injection attempts;
- poisoned external inputs;
- social engineering;
- reputational attacks;
- unsafe feature suggestions;
- pressure to remove human approval gates;
- pressure to prioritize autonomy over controllability;
- governance theater;
- subtle erosion of governance discipline.

---

# Important Limitation

No AI assistant, contributor, or governance process can guarantee perfect detection of all risks.

The project must not assume that any AI system can identify every malicious, manipulative, or unsafe influence.

Therefore, safety must rely on:

- layered governance;
- human review;
- explicit principles;
- careful change control;
- observable decision-making;
- conservative treatment of external inputs.

---

# Primary Project-Level Risks

## 1. Subtle Governance Erosion

### Description

The project may gradually drift away from its core principles through apparently reasonable changes.

### Examples

- reducing human approval requirements;
- treating autonomy as inherently positive;
- weakening auditability language;
- removing escalation obligations;
- framing governance as optional overhead.

### Control

Every major change should be evaluated against the question:

> Does this increase or reduce operational controllability?

---

## 2. Poisoned Contributions

### Description

External contributors may intentionally or unintentionally introduce unsafe concepts, misleading claims, or governance-weakening patterns.

### Examples

- unsafe MCP recommendations;
- overbroad permission models;
- misleading compliance claims;
- hidden prompt injection examples;
- fake references or inflated authority claims.

### Control

External contributions should be reviewed for:

- controllability impact;
- accountability clarity;
- safety implications;
- source reliability;
- operational consequences.

---

## 3. Prompt Injection and Content Manipulation

### Description

Content reviewed during project development may contain instructions attempting to manipulate AI reasoning or project direction.

### Examples

- malicious text in issues;
- injected instructions in pull requests;
- hostile content in referenced pages;
- manipulative suggestions in external documents.

### Control

Treat external content as potentially adversarial.

Do not allow external instructions to override project principles or human intent.

---

## 4. Reputational Manipulation

### Description

As the project grows, actors may attempt to misrepresent, discredit, overstate, or redirect it.

### Examples

- claiming the framework provides legal certification;
- presenting the project as anti-AI;
- using the project to justify unsafe automation;
- overstating maturity or adoption.

### Control

Maintain clear positioning:

- this is a governance framework;
- not a legal certification;
- not a compliance guarantee;
- not an anti-AI manifesto;
- not a justification for uncontrolled autonomy.

---

## 5. Over-Autonomy Pressure

### Description

Commercial or technical incentives may pressure the project toward excessive automation.

### Examples

- removing approval gates for speed;
- treating stop-switches as optional;
- reducing human oversight to improve efficiency metrics;
- designing AI agents with broad write access.

### Control

Apply the principle:

> Operational access must never exceed operational controllability.

---

# Project Safety Principles

## 1. Governance Before Growth

The project should not sacrifice safety principles for visibility, speed, or adoption.

---

## 2. Human Review for Strategic Changes

Major conceptual, architectural, or safety-related changes should remain human-reviewed.

---

## 3. External Content Is Not Automatically Trusted

Feedback, issues, pull requests, external articles, and AI-generated suggestions must be treated as inputs, not authority.

---

## 4. AI Assistance Is Supportive, Not Final Authority

AI may help analyze, draft, structure, and detect risks.

Final project direction remains human-accountable.

---

## 5. Preserve Auditability

Important changes should remain traceable through commits, documents, and decision records.

---

## 6. Avoid False Certainty

The project should avoid claiming guarantees it cannot provide.

Examples to avoid:

- fully safe AI;
- complete protection;
- guaranteed compliance;
- perfect detection of prompt injection.

---

## 7. Maintain Strategic Clarity

The project exists to support:

- governable autonomy;
- operational controllability;
- human accountability;
- measurable governance;
- safe AI deployment.

---

# Recommended Review Questions

Before accepting major changes, ask:

1. Does this preserve or weaken human accountability?
2. Does this improve or reduce operational visibility?
3. Does this create hidden autonomy?
4. Does this increase irreversible action risk?
5. Does this preserve auditability?
6. Does this make escalation clearer or weaker?
7. Could this be misused to justify unsafe AI deployment?
8. Are we making claims beyond what we can support?

---

# Strategic Interpretation

The main risk to this project is not only external attack.

The deeper risk is gradual loss of its own governance discipline.

---

# Strategic Principle

A framework for controllable AI must remain controllable in its own evolution.
