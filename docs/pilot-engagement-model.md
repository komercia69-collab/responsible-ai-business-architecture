# Pilot Engagement Model

## Responsible AI Business Architecture

> AI integration is not a plugin installation.
> It is a socio-technical architecture change.

---

## Purpose

This document defines a structured model for engaging organizations in controlled AI pilot projects.

The objective is not maximum automation.

The objective is measurable business value under controlled governance conditions.

---

# Core Principle

A successful AI pilot is not defined by how much autonomy is deployed.

A successful AI pilot is defined by whether the organization remains:

- operationally stable;
- financially controllable;
- accountable;
- auditable;
- governable.

---

# Pilot Lifecycle

| Phase | Objective |
|---|---|
| 1. Discovery | Understand business context |
| 2. Readiness Assessment | Evaluate maturity and risk |
| 3. Scope Definition | Define safe pilot boundaries |
| 4. Governance Design | Define controls and escalation |
| 5. Pilot Deployment | Controlled execution |
| 6. Monitoring | Measure impact and risk |
| 7. Review | Evaluate outcomes |
| 8. Scale or Stop | Expand, redesign, or terminate |

---

# 1. Discovery Phase

## Objective

Understand the organization before proposing automation.

## Key Questions

- What business problem exists?
- Which workflows are unstable?
- Which tasks are repetitive?
- Where are delays or bottlenecks?
- Which risks already exist without AI?

## Deliverables

- business context summary;
- stakeholder map;
- initial process inventory.

---

# 2. Readiness Assessment Phase

## Objective

Determine whether the organization is prepared for AI adoption.

## Evaluation Areas

- process maturity;
- data quality;
- infrastructure stability;
- governance capability;
- organizational readiness.

## Deliverables

- readiness score;
- governance gap analysis;
- risk classification.

---

# 3. Scope Definition Phase

## Objective

Define safe and measurable pilot boundaries.

## Scope Rules

Pilot projects should:

- begin with low or moderate risk workflows;
- avoid irreversible autonomous actions;
- maintain human approval points;
- use measurable operational metrics;
- include rollback capability.

## Deliverables

- pilot scope;
- approved workflows;
- excluded workflows;
- success criteria.

---

# 4. Governance Design Phase

## Objective

Establish operational controls before deployment.

## Required Governance Components

- AI permission matrix;
- escalation policy;
- stop-switch protocol;
- audit logging;
- owner dashboard;
- high-risk action restrictions.

## Deliverables

- governance architecture;
- escalation contacts;
- approval workflow;
- incident response process.

---

# 5. Pilot Deployment Phase

## Objective

Deploy AI capabilities in a controlled environment.

## Deployment Principles

- gradual rollout;
- limited autonomy;
- monitored execution;
- reversible actions where possible;
- human override availability.

## Deliverables

- active pilot;
- operational monitoring;
- incident tracking.

---

# 6. Monitoring Phase

## Objective

Measure operational impact continuously.

## Key Metrics

| Area | Example Metrics |
|---|---|
| Financial | cost reduction, margin impact |
| Operational | throughput, SLA compliance |
| Customer | complaints, satisfaction |
| Governance | escalations, overrides |
| AI Quality | hallucinations, correction rate |

## Deliverables

- weekly governance review;
- operational dashboard;
- risk reports.

---

# 7. Review Phase

## Objective

Evaluate whether the pilot produced sustainable value.

## Evaluation Questions

- Did operational stability improve?
- Did governance remain effective?
- Did human workload improve safely?
- Did customer trust remain stable?
- Were incidents manageable?

## Deliverables

- pilot evaluation report;
- governance findings;
- scale recommendation.

---

# 8. Scale or Stop Phase

## Objective

Decide whether to expand, redesign, or terminate the pilot.

## Expansion Conditions

AI capability should scale only when:

- governance remained stable;
- auditability remained intact;
- escalation worked correctly;
- risks remained controlled;
- business value was measurable.

## Stop Conditions

Pilot should stop when:

- operational visibility is lost;
- escalation repeatedly fails;
- trust deteriorates;
- risk exposure becomes unclear;
- human accountability weakens.

## Deliverables

- scale roadmap;
- redesign plan;
- retirement decision.

---

# Recommended Pilot Constraints

Early pilots should avoid:

- autonomous financial transfers;
- legal commitments;
- employment decisions;
- uncontrolled customer communication;
- security policy modification;
- self-modifying AI permissions.

---

# Stakeholder Roles

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| Business Owner | Strategic accountability |
| Governance Lead | Risk and compliance oversight |
| Technical Lead | Infrastructure and integration |
| Operations Lead | Workflow supervision |
| End Users | Operational feedback |
| AI System | Task execution within limits |

---

# Minimum Governance Standard

No AI pilot should proceed without:

- defined approval boundaries;
- rollback capability;
- audit logging;
- escalation process;
- assigned human accountability.

---

# Strategic Principle

AI pilots should not optimize for maximum autonomy.

They should optimize for sustainable controllability.
