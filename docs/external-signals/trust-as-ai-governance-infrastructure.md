## Summary

This external signal frames trust in AI not as messaging, public relations, or policy language, but as operational governance infrastructure.

The useful signal for RABA is narrow:

```text
Trust in AI becomes meaningful only when authority, approval, evidence, escalation, and accountability are operationally visible.
```

RABA treats this as an external market-context signal only.

This note does not rely on detailed legal, regulatory, certification, or product-specific claims from the source material. Those claims require separate verification before any stronger use.

## RABA Interpretation

RABA can interpret this signal as an indication that AI governance is moving from abstract principles toward operational systems.

In RABA terms:

```text
AI capability does not create business authority.
AI output does not create accountability.
Technical control does not replace governance ownership.
```

A responsible AI workflow requires explicit mechanisms for:

```text
who may act;
who may approve;
who must escalate;
what must be logged;
who remains accountable.
```

This aligns with the RABA view that responsibility must be visible, traceable, and governable inside Human-AI workflows.

## Guardrail Boundary

The source material discusses technical control layers and guardrails. RABA must preserve a strict boundary between technical control and governance ownership.

```text
Guardrails can enforce constraints.
Guardrails do not own responsibility.
Validation is not accountability.
Trace is not authority.
Policy enforcement is not governance ownership.
```

This distinction prevents technical enforcement from being mistaken for responsibility.

A technical system may support governance by enforcing rules, blocking unsafe paths, producing logs, or surfacing evidence.

It must not silently become the accountable owner of the decision or its consequences.
