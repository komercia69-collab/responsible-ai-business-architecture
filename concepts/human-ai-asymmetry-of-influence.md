## Purpose

This concept describes a structural imbalance in human-AI interaction.

A human being approaches an AI system with a local question, limited context, personal constraints, emotional state, time pressure, and finite judgment capacity.

The AI system responds with a signal generated through massive technical infrastructure, aggregated training data, statistical patterns, platform-level assumptions, safety layers, product design choices, and millions of comparable interactions.

On the surface, the interaction may look like a simple exchange:

> human asks → AI answers

But structurally, it is an interaction between:

> a local human request  
> and  
> a globally scaled technological response.

This creates a deep asymmetry of influence.

## Core Principle

Human-AI interaction is not symmetrical.

The human may influence a single prompt, a local decision, or a limited workflow.

The AI-enabled system may influence human decisions, attention, knowledge, organizational behavior, institutional trust, labor processes, economic incentives, and social patterns at scale.

For this reason, responsible AI governance must protect the human side of the interaction from being overpowered by the scale, speed, fluency, confidence, and apparent authority of the system.

## Why This Matters for RABA

RABA does not treat AI output as neutral information.

AI output can become:

- a recommendation;
- a decision frame;
- an authority signal;
- a behavioral nudge;
- a scope expansion;
- a hidden commitment;
- an execution trigger;
- a justification for organizational action.

If this influence is not governed, the human may appear to remain responsible while the system has already shaped the decision space.

This is especially important when the human or organization is under pressure, lacks expertise, faces economic constraints, or needs rapid action.

## Balance Is Not Equality

The goal is not to pretend that the human and AI system are equal actors.

They are not.

The goal is to create a protective balance that compensates for structural asymmetry.

This balance should preserve:

- human agency;
- human comprehension;
- decision ownership;
- responsibility visibility;
- scope awareness;
- awareness of assumptions;
- ability to slow down or reject AI-shaped action.

## Governance Requirements

A RABA-compatible system should include mechanisms that reduce harmful asymmetry.

### 1. Assumption Visibility

The system should make important assumptions visible when they affect the recommendation, decision, or action boundary.

### 2. Confidence Humility

The system should avoid presenting probabilistic outputs as absolute certainty.

### 3. Human Agency Protection

The system should preserve the human’s ability to understand, question, delay, reject, or re-scope AI output.

### 4. Influence Friction

For consequential actions, the system should slow the path from AI output to execution.

### 5. Context Imbalance Detection

If a local or simple user request may lead to large consequences, the system should raise the governance level.

### 6. No Hidden Authority Transfer

AI output must not silently become authorization, approval, commitment, or execution permission.

### 7. Fresh Confirmation

If the scope, risk, target, or consequence changes, previous human confirmation must not be reused as authority for the new action.

## Relationship to Active Confirmation Interlock

The Active Confirmation Interlock is one practical mechanism for reducing Human-AI Asymmetry of Influence.

It helps prevent a fluent AI-generated action summary from becoming a symbolic or accidental approval.

It requires the Human Owner to actively re-engage with the exact action before execution.

However, the interlock is only one mechanism.

The broader asymmetry problem begins earlier: at the level of intent, design, interface, workflow, and organizational pressure.

## Relationship to Social Responsibility Layer

The Social Responsibility Layer asks:

> Who can carry responsibility for the safe direction of human-AI interaction?

Human-AI Asymmetry of Influence explains why such a layer is necessary.

Because the interaction is structurally imbalanced, society and organizations need people, roles, institutions, and governance structures capable of protecting the human side of the interaction.

## Draft Formulation

> The human-AI interaction is structurally asymmetric.  
> A human brings a local question, limited context, and finite judgment capacity.  
> The AI system returns a signal generated through massive infrastructure, aggregated data, statistical patterns, platform assumptions, and millions of comparable interactions.  
> Therefore, responsible AI governance must protect the human side of the interaction from being overpowered by the scale, speed, fluency, and apparent authority of the system.
